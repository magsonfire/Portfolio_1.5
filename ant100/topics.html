Topics List

Pick any one of the following three essay topics for your assignment:

  • Evolutionary Topic (EVO)
  • Archaeology Topic (ARC)
  • Social-Cultural-Linguistic Topic A (SCL A)
  • Social-Cultural-Linguistic Topic B (SCL B)

The readings provided below are required readings for this assignment. You must use at least one of the scholarly readings provided with each topic for your essay.

Evolutionary Anthropology (EVO)

Professor Lehman argued that evolutionary anthropology refutes the existence of biological races in modern humans. Nonetheless, media and politicians constantly refer to human races, whether in the context of discrimination issues or in directed benefit plans to assist people of a “visible minority.” Researchers in some scientific disciplines claim to be able to determine human racial features and thus substantiate the notion of human race concepts. For example, forensic anthropologists often seek to determine racial classifications based on skeletal analyses of human remains. Your essay should explicitly answer the following question: can forensic anthropologists accurately and precisely determine racial features by analyzing skeletal remains?

Scholarly Reading

  • Ousley, S., Jantz, R., & Freid, D. (2009). Understanding race and human variation: why forensic anthropologists are good at identifying race. American Journal of Physical Anthropology, 139, (1), 68-76.
  • Relethford, J. H. (2009). Race and global patterns of phenotypic variation. American Journal of Physical Anthropology, 139, (1), 16-22.

Popular Article

  • Sussman, R.W. (2014). There is no such thing as race. Newsweek Magazine. Tech & Science report.

Archaeology (ARC)

Why did ancient hunter-gatherers make rock art? For tens of thousands of years, people around the world have painted and engraved a wide range of subjects on rock surfaces, often in caves. The images are compelling, but it is difficult to understand why they were created, and more specifically what they meant to the people who originally made and viewed them. Were they intended as hunting magic, as parts of initiation rituals, as accounts of shamans’ (religious specialists’) supernatural journeys, or something completely different? Of course, it is likely that rock art had a diversity of meanings in different times and places, and the world’s regions have seen a range of interpretations of local rock art. For this essay, choose one site or region, and answer the question: why did ancient people make rock art? Your essay should include a review of the range of interpretations that have been offered for the meaning of rock art, but also define which interpretation(s) offer the most convincing explanation. Restrict your answers to hunter- gatherer (pre-agricultural) societies

Scholarly Reading

  • Lewis-Williams, J. D. (2012). Rock art and shamanism. In J. McDonald & P. Veth (eds.), A Companion to Rock Art (pp. 17-33). Chichester: Blackwell Publishing Ltd.

Popular Article

  • Thurman, J. (2008). First Impressions: What does the world’s oldest art say about us? The New Yorker, June 23, 2008. Retrieved from http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2008/06/23/first-impressions

Social-Cultural-Linguistic Anthropology (SCL A)

In recent decades, international migration has become a major social policy challenge. Millions of people, largely from the former colonies of the West, have streamed not only into states established to receive immigrants such as Canada or the United States, but also countries that had not been targeted by migrants in recent times, such as Spain. What changes does immigration bring about in such countries? And what can socio-cultural anthropologists do to help their immigration policies? These are the issues you will tackle in this essay, expressing your point of view with close reference to the article by Traci Abraham. You may limit your discussion to Spain, or you may expand it by comparison to other areas. For example, you may make a comparison with asylum seekers in Canada, or with the recent influx of Germany-bound migrants to Europe.Before the British came to Australia, the aboriginal people whose language varied widely across the continent did not make the kind of sharp language boundaries that linguists invented for them. Nor did they make the kind of clear differences between one tribe and another that the British and Australian governments later imposed on them, often on the basis of alleged language differences. These linguistic and tribal distinctions are now contested by some aboriginal people.

Scholarly Reading

  • Abraham, T. (2014). Using ethnography to inform policy and programs for the integration of immigrants: A case study in Andalusia, Spain. Journal of International Migration and Integration 15 (1), 117-134.

Popular Articles

  • Black Africans in Tangiers targeted by racist raids and machete attacks. France 24, February 9, 2014.

Social-Cultural-Linguistic Anthropology (SCL B)

What are the unconscious rules for using emoticons? How does their use relate to spoken and written English? Answer these questions with close reference to the required article and the popular media. Consider what may have changed between the article’s publication in 2011, and today. Note: Focus on the form and use of emoticons. Do not comment on whether using them is good or bad (for you, for the language, or for society).

Scholarly Reading

  • Garrison, A., Remley, D., Thomas, P., & Wierszewski, E. (2011). Conventional faces: Emoticons in instant messaging discourse. Computers and Composition, 28 (2), 112-25.

Popular Articles

  • Kennedy, P. (2012). Who made that :) emoticon? The New York Times Magazine (Nov 25, 2012), 20.
  • A list of Japanese emoji
